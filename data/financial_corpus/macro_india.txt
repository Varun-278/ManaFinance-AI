MACROECONOMIC OVERVIEW OF INDIA 

1. STRUCTURE OF THE INDIAN ECONOMY
India is a services-driven economy, with the sector contributing ~54% of Gross Value Added (GVA).
Industry contributes ~28%, and agriculture contributes ~18%. Economic performance is influenced
by monsoon variability, domestic demand cycles, global commodity prices, and capital formation trends.
Private consumption accounts for ~57% of GDP, making India one of the most consumption-led
large economies globally.

2. GDP GROWTH DYNAMICS
GDP growth is determined by a mix of investment spending, government expenditure, external demand,
and private consumption. India’s medium-term growth potential is estimated between 6–7%. 
Key long-term growth enablers include infrastructure investment, digitalization, demographic dividend,
financial inclusion, and export competitiveness.

3. INFLATION MECHANICS
Inflation in India is officially measured using the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
Major inflation drivers:
 - Demand-pull inflation: caused by high consumer spending, credit growth, or fiscal expansion.
 - Cost-push inflation: caused by supply bottlenecks, global commodity shocks, and logistics issues.
 - Imported inflation: India imports ~85% of its crude oil, making fuel prices a major inflation input.
RBI’s inflation target is 4% ± 2%.

4. MONETARY POLICY — RBI’S ROLE
The Reserve Bank of India maintains price stability while supporting economic growth.
The Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meets every 2 months.
Key policy tools include:
 - Repo Rate: the rate at which the RBI lends to commercial banks.
 - Reverse Repo Rate: the rate at which banks park excess funds with the RBI.
 - Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR): portion of bank deposits to be held with the RBI.
 - Open Market Operations (OMO): liquidity adjustments by buying/selling government securities.
 - Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR): minimum liquid assets banks must maintain.

5. FISCAL POLICY — GOVERNMENT’S ROLE
India’s fiscal policy is executed through taxation, government expenditure, subsidies, and
capital investments. The long-term fiscal consolidation roadmap aims to bring the fiscal deficit
to ~4.5% by FY26. Key expenditure drivers include infrastructure, defence, rural development,
and welfare schemes.

6. EXTERNAL SECTOR & TRADE POSITION
India’s external sector is shaped by:
 - Current Account Deficit (CAD)
 - Foreign Exchange Reserves (over USD 600 billion)
 - Services exports: IT, ITeS, consulting, financial services
 - Remittances: India is the world’s largest remittance recipient (~USD 100 billion)
 - Exchange rate fluctuations and capital flows
Trade vulnerabilities include crude oil dependence and global recession exposure.

7. FINANCIAL MARKETS
India’s capital markets include equity markets (NIFTY50, SENSEX), bond markets, money markets,
and currency markets.

Equity Markets:
NIFTY50 represents 50 large-cap companies across major sectors and acts as a barometer of
market sentiment.

Bond Markets:
Government securities (G-Secs) dominate the bond ecosystem. Bond yields move in response to
inflation, monetary policy, and government borrowing.

8. STRUCTURAL & POLICY REFORMS
Key structural reforms influencing macroeconomic performance:
 - GST (Goods and Services Tax)
 - Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC)
 - Make in India manufacturing push
 - National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP)
 - Digital Public Infrastructure (UPI, Aadhaar, ONDC)
 - Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes

These reforms aim to increase productivity, formalize the economy, and boost manufacturing.

9. MACRO RISKS TO INDIA’S ECONOMY
Major risk factors:
 - Crude oil price spikes affecting inflation and the current account
 - Global recession affecting exports and capital markets
 - Climate shocks affecting agriculture output
 - Geopolitical tensions impacting supply chains
 - Rupee volatility influencing import costs and inflation

10. OUTLOOK FOR THE NEXT DECADE
India’s macroeconomic foundation remains strong due to:
 - Large domestic market
 - Rapid digital adoption
 - Young labor force
 - Formalization and financialization of the economy
 - Infrastructure expansion

Sustaining 6–7%+ growth will require improvements in labor participation,
manufacturing competitiveness, skill development, and long-term policy stability.
