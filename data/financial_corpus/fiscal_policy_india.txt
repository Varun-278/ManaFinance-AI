FISCAL POLICY IN INDIA 

1. OBJECTIVE OF FISCAL POLICY
Fiscal policy manages economic cycles, boosts long-term growth, promotes equity, and stabilises
inflation. The government uses expenditure, taxation, and borrowing to achieve macroeconomic goals.

2. FISCAL DEFICIT & CONSOLIDATION
Fiscal deficit = Total expenditure – Total revenue  
India’s fiscal deficit target:
- ~4.5% of GDP by FY26
- FRBM Act governs long-term consolidation

3. REVENUE STRUCTURE
Government receipts include:
- Direct taxes: income tax, corporate tax
- Indirect taxes: GST, excise
- Non-tax revenue: dividends, PSU profits
- Capital receipts: disinvestment, borrowings

4. EXPENDITURE STRUCTURE
a) Revenue expenditure  
   - Subsidies (food, fuel, fertilizer)  
   - Defense revenue expenditure  
   - Salaries, pensions  
b) Capital expenditure  
   - Infrastructure: roads, railways, ports  
   - Defense capital procurement  
   - Digital public infrastructure

5. FISCAL MULTIPLIER EFFECT
Capital expenditure has a multiplier > 2, meaning investment creates sustained demand.
Revenue expenditure multiplier is < 1.

6. PUBLIC DEBT MANAGEMENT
India’s debt-to-GDP ~ 84%.  
Managed mainly by RBI through:
- G-sec auctions
- Switch operations
- Buybacks
- Yield curve management (YCC-like signals)

7. FEDERAL FINANCE
Centre–state fiscal relations governed by:
- Finance Commission (every 5 years)
- GST Council
- State borrowing limits

8. AUTOMATIC STABILISERS
Examples:
- Progressive taxation
- Unemployment insurance (limited in India)
- Food security schemes

9. CHALLENGES
- High subsidy burden
- Low tax-to-GDP ratio (~11%)
- Rising interest payments
- Limited fiscal headroom for counter-cyclical stimulus

10. STRATEGIC PRIORITIES
- Infrastructure push
- Green economy spending
- Formalization incentives
- Fiscal consolidation
